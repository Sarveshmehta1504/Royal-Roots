BWP vs BWR Plywood: What's the Difference?

March 10, 2026
4 min read

When you're designing beautiful, long-lasting interiors or selecting furniture materials, you will inevitably hear carpenters and interior designers throw around terms like BWP and BWR plywood. To the untrained ear, these might sound like the same thing—but making the wrong choice could cut your furniture's lifespan in half.

At Royal Roots Ply India, we don't just sell plywood; we educate our creators. Here is the definitive guide to understanding the difference between BWP and BWR plywood.

1. What is BWP Plywood? (Boiling Water Proof)

BWP stands for Boiling Water Proof. It is also commonly known as Marine Grade Plywood and is governed by the IS:710 specification from the Bureau of Indian Standards.

  • The Test: To be certified as true BWP, a plywood sheet must be able to withstand boiling water for 72 continuous hours without the plies delaminating (separating).
  • The Resin: It is manufactured using undiluted Phenol Formaldehyde (PF) synthetic resin, giving it its incredible waterproof properties.
  • Best For: Areas with extreme moisture exposure. Kitchen sink units, bathroom vanities, exterior wall cladding, and actual marine applications like boat building.
If water is going to touch it frequently, you need BWP. That's why our Royal Roots Platinum grade is 100% IS:710 certified BWP.

2. What is BWR Plywood? (Boiling Water Resistant)

BWR stands for Boiling Water Resistant. It falls under the IS:303 specification (which it shares with standard MR or Moisture Resistant plywood).

  • The Test: BWR plywood can withstand boiling water for about 8 hours before delamination occurs.
  • The Resin: It also uses Phenol Formaldehyde resin, but typically in an extended or diluted form compared to BWP.
  • Best For: Areas with high humidity but no direct water contact. Living room furniture, bedroom wardrobes, and overhead kitchen cabinets (away from the sink).

3. The Royal Roots Verdict: Which Should You Choose?

While BWR is perfectly adequate for most interior residential needs, modern homes often require more robust solutions.

If you are building a modular kitchen, we strongly advise using BWP (IS:710) for the base cabinets—especially under the sink—to protect against inevitable plumbing leaks and mopping. You can safely use BWR for the overhead cabinets to optimize your budget.
